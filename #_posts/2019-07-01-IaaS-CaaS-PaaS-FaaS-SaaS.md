# IaaS, CaaS, PaaS, FaaS and SaaS
## Introduction
Once upon a time only physical machines existed: work laptops, home desktops and business servers on-prem. Then virtualization came along and made it possible to split those physical machines into smaller virtual machines; Each was isolated from the others, they could communicate through same network and be started, stopped and copied easily. When public clouds became popular those virtual machines got new capabilities: now you weren't limited to a physical server on-prem that you bought and owned, but you could just start a new VM with 1 core and 2 gb, or two of them, or one with double the capacity, or one hundred of them. And buy and own them per hour. And not worry about hardware support/refresh. So although VMs on-prem in public clouds are comparable, VMs in (proper) public clouds show benefits.

Docker helped us all by bundling existing process isolation capabilities in Linux and made them accessible through container technology. A machine now just needed a container engine and we could start new instances of an application as a new process, instead of starting a complete virtual machine with its overhead of size, maintenance, operating system license, etc.
